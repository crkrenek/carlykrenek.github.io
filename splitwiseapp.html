Splitwise App Redesign | Date

Description

For this project, I was tasked with redesigning an existing online service or app. My redesign focused on a key feature of the app, Splitwise, an expense sharing app.

My Roles

  • UX Strategy
  • Market Research
  • Interaction Design
  • Prototyping
  • Usability Testing

Tools Used

  • Figma

Goals

Business

The business goal of this project was to grow the number of Splitwise app users to 100MM+ in a year's time.

User

The user goal for this work was to be able to easily and quickly add expenses and split them among a group.

Strategies & Objectives

Strategy 1

Launch a redesign of the Splitwise app to increase app engagement and user retention.

Objective 1

Optimize the amount of time it takes to "add an expense" by reducing the number of user actions and changing the process to begin with the most important actions first.

Objective 2

Create a dashboard to orient and inform users about what they owe/are owed upon opening the app.

Strategy 2

Update the aesthetics of the app to give it a more appropriate thematic interface that feels more modern while maintaining the overall message of the system.

Objective 1

Reduce the number of colors used throughout the app to eliminate accessibility issues and over-stimulation.

Persona & Scenario - Allison Adams

Group Trip

Allison is a teacher that loves to travel. She prefers to travel with friends to make it more affordable, fun, and safe.

She and a group of her friends are going on a trip to Destin, FL and they will have several shared expenses they will need to keep track of and split fairly. In the past, her friends haven't always paid her back for travel expenses and sometimes things weren't split correctly. In order to prevent this from happening, they are planning to use the Splitwise app to track and split expenses throughout the trip.

The Original Task Flow

While the flow consisted of an acceptable number of clicks, the flow did not seem logical and important options regarding the way expenses would be split seemed to be hidden. User reviews indicated that navigation, redundancy, and hidden paths left them feeling that the “add expense” flow was confusing and lacked efficiency.

Step 1

Users click on the green “add expense” button to begin the process.

Step 2

Then they select who the expense should be split between. They can select friends or groups.

Step 3

Click to enter the name/description of the expense and the total amount of the expense. Users can click on the “you” button to change the payer. They can also click on the “equally” button to change the way the expense should be split.

Step 4

Users can select a different payer or add multiple payers.

Step 5

They can change the split option and enter the details to complete the split accordingly.

The Improved Task Flow

While I didn’t feel the number of steps or clicks in the original flow were too lengthy, I did want to make the flow more visible and familiar, and less confusing. I began sketching some wireframes to fix the issues identified above. I kept the flow relatively similar, but I wanted the split options to be visible during the flow without users clicking on the button. Some users may not have known there were other split options present. This was the main difference, but I also wanted a dashboard home page that would orient the user and offer them a clear path to adding an expense.

Wireframing, Prototyping, & Testing

Once I had identified the changes I wanted to make to improve the current issues, I created wireframes to illustrate the new flow. I began by researching dashboard UI patterns. After reading user reviews that were asking for a way to quickly see what they owe and who owes them, I decided to incorporate that into the dashboard home page. During testing, users revealed that the wording used on this page was a bit confusing. They also pointed out that they weren’t initially sure what the plus sign button was for. This would become something I fixed in the next iteration.

Logical Form Flow

The first version of this interface had users selecting who the expense would be split between first. Some users expressed that they might not know who is splitting at the time they enter the expense. Rather, they may need to enter the “split between” information later. This informed my decision to begin the form with the expense description, total amount, and the payer.

During usability testing, users indicated that the “who paid” section was confusing, because they didn’t understand the icon labeled “me”.

De-Cluttered Split Page

Incorporating this split options page as part of the flow made these options more visible to the user. This way they knew they had other options if they needed a more complex split option.

I removed the elephant graphics and extra information on this page to highlight the key information: the split option and who it is being split between. The design decision here was to alleviate the users’ feelings of being overwhelmed by too much information.

High Fidelity Design

In the final design, I incorporated feedback gained from my usability testing. The wording in the dashboard was changed to “Friends owe you” and “You owe” to make it more clear for users. During testing, there was confusion on where to click to add a new expense. While users undestood the plus symbol indicated the addition of something, they weren’t sure what exactly would be added. Incorporating that feedback into the final design, I added an “add expense” label in the navigation bar to indicate the button initiated the process.

The decision was made to go with a more monochrome color palette keeping with the original green color, but eliminating many of the icon colors. The red was used as an accent color acting as a warning. It draws the users attention upon opening the app so they can quickly identify what their outstanding balances are and take action to pay them.

Uniform Expense Form

Having a streamlined expense form with the necessary fields formatted the same offers users a familiar user interface for entering information. This decreases the potential for users to become confused or wonder if they have filled out everything on the page.

The visual pattern was improved to create a linear path that makes filling out the form more efficient.

Simplified Visible Split Options

The split expense option was typically defaulted to “equal” in the original design. However, many users need more complex ways to split their bills. In the original design, the split options were not visible to users and may have been overlooked by many.

This screen allows every user to see all the split options. If users need clarification on what each split option means they can find it via the question mark icon. This eliminates the need for screen space being taken up with paragraphs of text and graphics that don’t connect with the user.

The collapsible feature keeps the design feeling minimal while eliminating the need for an individual screen for each split option.
